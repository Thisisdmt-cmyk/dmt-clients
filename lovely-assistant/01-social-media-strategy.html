What's Working

Instagram Is Your Biggest Platform

At 874 followers with 175 posts, Instagram is where your largest audience lives. The artistic visual identity you've built — illustrated artwork, considered aesthetic — suits Instagram perfectly. You're following 401 accounts too, which means you're actually part of a community, not just broadcasting into the void. This is a genuine strength.

Active & Consistent Posting

You're showing up. That matters more than most people realise. 175 posts means you've built a body of content that tells a story. New followers can scroll back and see a real band with a real history.

Proper Facebook Page

577 followers on a proper Facebook page (not a personal profile) is solid. Facebook still matters for events, local gig promotion, and reaching an audience who'll actually buy tickets and vinyl. Your page at facebook.com/lovelyassistantmusic is set up correctly and active.

YouTube Presence

You've got music videos up on @lovelyassistant, including live performance videos. Video content is the hardest to create and you're already doing it. That's a real head start.

Smart Bio Setup

Your Instagram bio already mentions sync representation (@chelseawood89) and support from Help Musicians, Generator NE, and Glasshouse ICM. That signals professionalism to anyone checking you out — press, promoters, playlist curators. Good instinct.

The Honest Picture

Instagram is bigger than Facebook but likely getting less strategic attention. That's your single biggest opportunity right now.

Where the Gaps Are

  • No TikTok presence — not necessarily a problem, but short-form video is where discovery happens now, and your live performances would translate brilliantly to that format
  • Cross-platform content could be more coordinated — different content on different platforms is fine, but big announcements and releases should land everywhere at the same time
  • Spotify monthly listeners (98) lag behind social following — this is common for live-focused acts, and the live album is a chance to close that gap
  • Link-in-bio could work harder — campsite.bio/lovelyassistant is functional, but it could be more strategically ordered with the most important link at the top

The Big Moment Ahead

The live album dropping spring 2026 is a proper content event. The mix is signed off, which means you can start building anticipation now. This is the biggest marketing moment you've had since "Fuck Love" launched in February 2025 — and this time you can plan the whole rollout in advance.

Prioritised Opportunities

High Priority

1. Go Instagram-First

Instagram is your biggest audience. You might want to treat it like your primary platform:

  • Stories daily — even just a rehearsal clip, a coffee at the community centre, a lyric scribbled on paper. Stories keep you visible without needing polished content.
  • Reels 2-3x per week — short clips of live performances, behind-the-scenes moments, even 15 seconds of a soundcheck. Reels are how Instagram shows you to new people.
  • Carousel posts for depth — album artwork deep dives, lyric breakdowns, gig photo sets. These get saved and shared more than single images.
  • Update your bio link order — when the live album pre-save goes live, make it the top link in your campsite.bio.
High Priority

2. Live Album Launch Campaign

You've got a genuine event coming. Here's a suggested timeline for rolling it out:

4-6 Weeks Before Release

Announce the live album. Share a behind-the-scenes photo from the recording. Post a short audio teaser — just 15 seconds of a track.

3-4 Weeks Before

Reveal the tracklist. Share stories from the recording sessions. Start a pre-save campaign if your distributor supports it.

2 Weeks Before

Drop the artwork. Share a full track or lead single. Post countdown content in Stories.

Release Week

Launch post across all platforms simultaneously. Go live on Instagram. Post daily Stories. Spotlight individual tracks over the week.

2-4 Weeks After

Behind-the-scenes video, fan reactions, live clips from the recording. Keep the conversation going — the album doesn't stop mattering after release day.

High Priority

3. Facebook Events for Every Gig

This is free and takes five minutes. Create a Facebook Event for every single gig — village hall shows on the Highlights Rural Touring Scheme, Cobalt in Newcastle, Hyde Park Book Club in Leeds, Last Train Home Festival, everything. Events get shared, they show up in local searches, and they give people a reason to engage with the page. Invite your followers to each one.

Medium Priority

4. Short-Form Video (TikTok / Reels)

You don't need to become TikTok creators. But short clips of live performances — especially those village hall shows — are gold for discovery. The aesthetic of a proper band playing in a community space is genuinely compelling content that most artists can't replicate.

  • Film 30-60 second clips at gigs (phone on a tripod works fine)
  • Post to Instagram Reels first, then cross-post to TikTok
  • Clips from the live album recording sessions would be perfect source material
  • No editing needed — raw, honest clips often outperform polished ones
Medium Priority

5. Cross-Platform Coordination

When you post something important, it's worth posting it everywhere on the same day. The format can change — a carousel on Instagram, a text post on Facebook, a thread on X — but the message should land at the same time. For the live album launch especially, coordinated timing makes a difference.

When You're Ready

6. Start an Email List

This might feel old-fashioned, but an email list is the only audience you truly own. A clipboard at the merch table at village hall gigs works. Even 50 genuine email subscribers who'll open every message is worth more than 500 passive followers. Services like Mailchimp are free for small lists. When the live album drops, you'll have a direct line to the people who care most.
