Artist Branding

Visual Identity Audit

Good news first: you already have a distinctive visual identity. The illustrated artwork style, the name "Lovely Assistant," the tagline "Quartet of Heck" — these are strong foundations that many bands never develop. This isn't about starting from scratch. It's about tightening what's already there.

What's Working

  • The name — "Lovely Assistant" is distinctive, memorable, and searchable. It doesn't sound like anyone else. That's more valuable than people realise.
  • "Quartet of Heck" — this tagline is perfect. It's funny, warm, self-aware, and it immediately tells you something about the band's personality. Use it everywhere.
  • Illustrated artwork style — your album and single artwork uses illustration rather than photography. This gives you a signature visual language that's immediately recognisable in a feed or playlist.
  • Tone of voice — your social media posts, bio text, and interviews all share a consistent tone: dry, warm, self-deprecating, genuine. This is your voice and it's distinctive.
  • Instagram grid — 175 posts means your grid tells a visual story. The mix of artwork, live shots, and behind-the-scenes content feels authentic.

What Varies

  • Photography quality — some images are strong, considered shots; others are quick phone photos. Both are fine, but the ratio matters. Your press/promo shots should be the strongest images you have.
  • Platform-to-platform consistency — the feel on Instagram vs Facebook vs Bandcamp vs the website may not be quite aligned. Different bios, different photos, slightly different tones.
  • Colour palette isn't formalised — your artwork uses warm, earthy tones that feel right for the music, but there's no defined set of colours that says "this is Lovely Assistant."
  • Typography — album artwork and social graphics may use different fonts. A consistent type choice (even just one or two fonts) would tie everything together.

Brand Strengths

Things You Don't Need to Change

Some things are already working well and should stay exactly as they are:

  • The name — perfect. Don't change it.
  • "Quartet of Heck" — keep using this. It's sticky.
  • "Defiantly without genre" — this is a strong positioning statement. It tells people what to expect (the unexpected) and it's honest.
  • The illustrated artwork approach — this is your visual signature. Keep it.
  • The community centre / village hall identity — this is genuine and distinctive. Don't polish it away.

Prioritised Opportunities

High Priority

1. Formalise a Colour Palette

You might want to pull a set of 5-6 colours from your existing artwork and use them consistently across everything — social media posts, flyers, the website, merch. This doesn't mean every post needs to match, but having a defined palette means everything feels connected.

A suggested palette based on your aesthetic is in the Assets tab. Use it as-is or adjust to your taste.

High Priority

2. Press Photo Consistency

For the live album release, it would be worth having one strong set of press photos taken — all four of you, consistent lighting and setting. These become the photos that go everywhere: Spotify, Apple Music, your website, press features, festival programmes. One good session lasts 1-2 years.

A brief for the shoot is in the Assets tab.

Medium Priority

3. Platform Consistency

A quick pass across all platforms to align bios, profile photos, and header images makes a bigger difference than you'd think. When a journalist or curator checks you out on multiple platforms, consistency signals professionalism. It doesn't take long:

  • Same profile photo everywhere (or at least same session/style)
  • Bios that tell the same story (adapted for platform length)
  • Consistent header/banner images where applicable
  • Same contact info and links on every platform

The consistency checklist is in the Assets tab.

Medium Priority

4. Live Album Artwork Direction

The live album is a chance to extend your visual identity. Some options to consider:

  • Continue the illustration style — maintain visual continuity with your previous releases
  • Use a live photograph — a strong shot from a gig, treated with your colour palette. This signals "live album" immediately.
  • Hybrid approach — an illustration that incorporates live elements (the venue, the audience, the band performing)

Whichever direction you go, keep it connected to the visual world you've already built. The cover should feel like it belongs alongside "Fuck Love" and "Greetings from the Pre-wave."

When You're Ready

5. Merch Direction

Your audience — village hall gig-goers, vinyl buyers, Bandcamp supporters — is exactly the kind of audience that buys merch. And with a live album on the way, there's a natural launch moment. Some thoughts on what would work for Lovely Assistant specifically are in the Assets tab.

Press Photo Brief

Photo Session Guide

If you're planning a photo session for the live album release, here's a brief you can share with a photographer:

Location ideas:

  • The community centre where you rehearse (authentic, distinctive, tells your story)
  • A village hall (connects to your touring circuit)
  • Industrial/urban Northumberland or Newcastle (your home territory)
  • A rural setting — fields, stone walls, farmland (connects to the Midlands recording of "Fuck Love")

Mood & tone:

  • Natural light preferred (golden hour is ideal)
  • Warm colour temperature — lean into the earthy tones of your palette
  • Relaxed, not posed. You're a band who work from a community centre, not a boy band. Capture that.
  • Some candid shots alongside the formal group shots

Deliverables to ask for:

  • 1 landscape group shot (for headers, press features, festival programmes)
  • 1 portrait/square group shot (for social media, Spotify profile)
  • Individual portraits of each member (for press articles, festival bios)
  • 5-10 candid/behind-the-scenes shots (for social media content)
  • All at high resolution (minimum 300dpi for print, 2000px+ for web)

Wardrobe:

  • Wear what you'd wear to a gig. Don't overthink it.
  • Avoid loud patterns or prominent logos (they date quickly and distract)
  • Earthy, muted tones look great in natural light and suit your palette

Merch Ideas

Merch That Suits Your Audience

Your audience skews towards people who go to village hall gigs, buy vinyl on Bandcamp, and appreciate craft. The merch should reflect that. Here are some ideas that would work specifically for Lovely Assistant:

  • Tote bags — with an illustrated design or "Quartet of Heck" text. Practical, affordable to produce, and your audience will actually use them. Perfect for village hall gigs where people are walking in with a bottle of wine.
  • Enamel pins — small, collectible, cheap to produce in small runs. A "Quartet of Heck" pin or a small illustrated design from your artwork.
  • T-shirts — keep the design understated. Your illustrated artwork style on a plain tee (heather grey, cream, or navy) would be strong. Avoid the "band t-shirt with dates on the back" approach unless it genuinely suits you.
  • Limited prints — your album artwork is illustration-based. Sell signed prints of the artwork. Cheap to produce, high perceived value, and your audience appreciates art.
  • Live album on physical format — given "Greetings from the Pre-wave" was released on limited 12" vinyl, your audience already buys physical. A limited vinyl run or even a limited CD run of the live album would sell at gigs.
